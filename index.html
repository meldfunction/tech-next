About this event

What you actually need to know before you pursue the federal market

The federal government is the largest buyer in the world — nearly $700 billion in contracts annually — and a growing share targets innovative small businesses and emerging technology companies. But the path from interested to under contract is unlike any market you have navigated.

Federal Market Ready is built for founders who want an honest, grounded orientation before investing years and resources chasing government work. You will leave with a clear-eyed self-assessment, a sequenced action plan, and real examples of companies that have walked this path — including some that failed.

What it's
actually
like

The federal government operates on logic that is fundamentally different from every market you have encountered. Understanding this before you enter is not optional.
Time horizon
18–36 month cycles are normal
A procurement cycle that takes 3 months in the private sector can take 18–36 months in federal. Founders who don't plan cash accordingly don't survive long enough to win.
Procurement reality
You sell before the RFP drops
By the time an RFP is published, the outcome is often 70–80% shaped by relationships built 12–18 months earlier. Finding an RFP and responding cold is usually too late.
Decision dynamics
The CO is not your customer
The program office is your customer. The contracting officer enforces rules. They are different people with different incentives. Confusing them is one of the most costly mistakes founders make.
Risk culture
Risk aversion is structural
Federal employees are personally accountable for procurement missteps in ways private buyers are not. Innovation is unfamiliar, and unfamiliar is risky. Expect friction by design, not hostility.

How to know if the government wants you

Most founders skip this entirely. These six questions should be answered honestly before you register for SAM.gov. If you cannot answer the first two clearly, the government is not your right next market move — and that is a resource allocation decision, not a failure.
01
Does a federal agency have a statutory mission your product directly serves?
Not "could conceivably be used by" — directly serves. Agencies buy what their authorizing legislation requires. If you have to stretch to explain the connection, the connection isn't there yet.
02
Is there already federal spending on the problem you solve?
Search USASpending.gov. If agencies are already paying someone else to solve your problem, that is a green light — a market exists. Silence means no appetite or a gap you would have to create.
03
Can your solution work within the government's actual constraints today?
Not your solution in ideal form. Your solution running on FedRAMP-authorized infrastructure, meeting ATO requirements, delivered by a team that can be badged in. Be honest about the gap between here and there.
04
Do you have 18–24 months of runway to pursue this channel?
Government is not a quick revenue move. Founders who treat it as a side bet while running low on cash lose. The ones who win have built it into their three-year plan from the start, with dedicated capacity.
05
Where does your technology sit on the TRL scale?
Government uses Technology Readiness Levels 1–9. Most agencies want TRL 6+ for procurement. SBIR and STTR are designed for TRL 1–5. Knowing your TRL determines which door you knock on first.
06
Are you solving a problem the agency already knows it has?
The government buys solutions to known problems. If you are creating problem awareness at the same time as selling your solution, your sales cycle has doubled. Meet agencies where they are.

The mindsets
that actually
matter

Mechanics can be learned from a checklist. These are harder. Founders who build durable federal businesses internalize all of them before the first contract. The ones who don't learn the expensive way.
01
Long game as strategy, not consolation
Founders who treat federal as a side bet lose. The ones who win treat 18-month cycles as normal and plan capacity and cash accordingly. Build this channel into your three-year plan or don't build it at all.
02
Compliance as competitive moat
Every requirement that feels like a tax is also a barrier that keeps underprepared competitors out. CMMC, FedRAMP, DCAA accounting — expensive to achieve and protective once you have them.
03
Mission lock is not optional
Federal agencies can identify transactional intent. Founders who build durable government relationships genuinely care about the mission of the agencies they serve. "We want revenue" is a strategy. "We want to solve X for agency Y" gets you in rooms.
04
Rejection is market intelligence
A lost proposal debrief is free consulting from your evaluators. Request every debrief. Mine every loss for evaluation criteria, scoring gaps, and competitor positioning. Losses are the curriculum.
05
You are building a business unit, not closing a deal
Federal BD requires different team structures, different tools, and different rhythms than commercial sales. Founders who treat it as just another channel chronically underinvest. Staff it, fund it, measure it as its own operation.
06
The fiscal year calendar is your editorial calendar
The government's year runs October 1 through September 30. Q4 (July–September) is when agencies spend aggressively. Q1 is slow. Time your pursuit activity around this calendar, not the calendar year.

The Runway Burn
22 months pursuing federal market, $1.8M burned, zero contracts won
What happened
A SaaS company with a strong commercial product in workforce analytics decided to pursue the federal market after losing two commercial enterprise deals to competitors with more established brands. The founding team reasoned that government contracts would provide stable, long-term revenue to compensate for commercial volatility.
They hired a consultant who advised them to respond to as many RFPs as possible. Over 22 months, they spent $1.8M on proposal development, compliance preparation, and two part-time BD hires — with no revenue to show for it. They ran out of cash and were acquired for parts at a fraction of their last valuation.
Warning signs that were present
No USASpending.gov research before entry. They had no evidence any agency was spending on workforce analytics prior to pursuing the market.
Every RFP they responded to was one they discovered after publication — never before. They were always too late in the relationship cycle.
They entered with 10 months of runway and expected to close a contract in 6. When that didn't happen, they kept investing from a position of financial pressure rather than strategic patience.
How to avoid this
Run the six-question diagnostic before spending a dollar. Validate market existence in USASpending before any other step.
Enter with 24 months of runway dedicated to federal BD, not 10. If you don't have it, don't enter yet.
Set a hard stop-loss: if you have not had a meaningful agency conversation (not a proposal submission — a real dialogue) within 6 months, reassess before spending more.
The Compliance Disqualification
Won a $2.3M DoD proposal, failed CMMC Level 2 assessment, contract terminated before start
What happened
A cybersecurity software firm won a competitive DoD contract after an 8-month proposal process. The award was announced publicly. They hired three people in anticipation of the work. Then the DoD required a CMMC Level 2 assessment as a condition of execution — something the contracting officer had mentioned during discussions but the team had not fully internalized.
The assessment revealed 47 practices out of compliance with NIST SP 800-171. Remediation was estimated at $400K and 9 months. The contracting officer declined to hold the award and re-competed the work. The company lost the contract, the three new hires, and the reputation hit from a public award reversal.
Warning signs that were present
The solicitation included DFARS clause 252.204-7012 requiring CMMC compliance. The team did not parse solicitation clauses before bidding.
They self-assessed their CMMC readiness as "probably fine" without a formal gap analysis. No C3PAO engagement prior to proposal submission.
How to avoid this
Conduct a formal CMMC gap analysis before pursuing any DoD work involving Controlled Unclassified Information. Budget 6–18 months and $100K–$500K for remediation if you are starting from scratch.
Parse every DFARS and FAR clause in solicitations before bidding. If you see 252.204-7012 or 252.204-7021 and are not CMMC-ready, do not submit.
Never hire in anticipation of an unexecuted award. Contract start is when you hire — not when you win.
The Cold RFP Trap
34 proposals submitted over 18 months, no agency relationships established, zero awards
What happened
A management consulting firm with strong commercial credentials decided to pursue federal work by identifying every open RFP in their domain and submitting competitive proposals. They used a commercial proposal writing firm and produced technically strong submissions. They received 34 declination notices over 18 months.
Post-mortem analysis of their debrief requests revealed a consistent pattern: they were scored competitively on technical merit but below average on past performance (which they didn't have) and management approach (which evaluators found generic). In most cases, the winner was a firm the agency had worked with before on something smaller.
Warning signs that were present
They had never attended a single industry day, responded to a Sources Sought, or had a market research conversation with any contracting office before submitting proposals.
Their "past performance" citations were commercial projects — relevant in domain but not familiar to federal evaluators who weight government work more heavily.
How to avoid this
Spend the first 6 months in relationship-building mode only. No proposals. Attend industry days, respond to Sources Sought, request CO market research meetings. Build name recognition before you need it.
Only submit proposals on solicitations where you have had at least one substantive pre-solicitation agency conversation. Cold proposals almost never win in competitive federal procurements.
Request debriefs on every declination. The pattern across 3–4 debriefs will tell you exactly what is missing. Do not guess — ask.
The Payment Cliff
Won a $12M subcontract, couldn't sustain the 90-day invoicing cycle, had to exit mid-performance
What happened
A small engineering firm landed a significant subcontract on a DoD prime worth $12M over two years — a major win that required them to double headcount. The teaming agreement specified Net-60 payment terms from the prime. Actual payment averaged 82 days. With two months of payroll to cover before each payment arrived, the firm exhausted its operating line of credit within 8 months.
They approached the prime for accelerated payments. The prime declined — their own prime contract had Net-45 terms from the government, and they were themselves cash-constrained. The small firm had to withdraw from the contract, triggering a cure notice and damaging their past performance record at a critical moment.
Warning signs that were present
They modeled cash flow assuming Net-30 payment based on the teaming agreement, not Net-60 or 90, which is standard in practice.
They did not secure a government contract line of credit or AR financing facility before executing the contract. They assumed the operating line would suffice.
How to avoid this
Model your cash flow assuming 90-day payment cycles, not 30 or 45. Government contracts almost always pay slower than commercial equivalents.
Before signing any subcontract, establish a government contract-specific line of credit or accounts receivable financing facility with a lender who understands federal contract receivables.
Negotiate prompt payment provisions into teaming agreements explicitly. Some primes will agree to Net-30 flow-down from the government's payment if you ask — but only if you negotiate it before signing.
The Wrong Door
2 years pursuing HHS with a DoD-native logistics product, no wins, restarted from scratch
What happened
A company that built AI-powered logistics optimization software — originally for commercial supply chains — decided to pursue HHS because a team member had a prior connection there. They spent two years attending HHS industry days, responding to HHS Sources Sought notices, and building relationships with HHS contracting officers.
The problem: HHS does not have a large logistics spend. Their procurement is dominated by health IT, grants management, and public health infrastructure — not supply chain optimization. The team's product was genuinely innovative, but it was solving a problem that HHS either had no budget for or handled through different mechanisms. Meanwhile, DLA (Defense Logistics Agency) and USTRANSCOM — the agencies with massive logistics spend — were ones the team had never approached.
Warning signs that were present
USASpending.gov showed minimal HHS spend in logistics-adjacent NAICS codes. They did not check this before beginning pursuit.
Agency selection was driven by a personal connection, not by market analysis. Relationships are valuable — but they need to be in an agency that actually buys what you sell.
How to avoid this
Use USASpending.gov to validate that your target agency is actually spending money in your domain before any pursuit investment. Filter by NAICS code and agency. Look for existing contracts to competitors as proof of market.
Agency selection should be driven by spending data first, connections second. A connection in the wrong agency will cost you more than it saves.
If you have expertise in logistics, supply chain, or operational optimization, the correct first call is DLA, USTRANSCOM, or Army Materiel Command — not civilian agencies regardless of personal connections.
